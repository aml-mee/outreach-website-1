§ 01 — The dispatch

One letter, one stamp,
one prospect — at first.

A single-page letter, written by hand and posted to the Group Chief Executive of one of the world's largest banks. No mailing list. No automation. Just two paragraphs of homework and a clear, modest proposal.

The recipient was the Group Chief Executive of a 170,000-person, 78-country bank. The sender was a small training firm in Buckinghamshire with one good idea and no introduction.

What got the letter through wasn't the channel — paper, in 2002. It was the two paragraphs at the top that proved the sender had actually read the bank's annual report and the CEO's interviews.

"You will not find this in any sales-automation tool. You will find it in a filing cabinet."

§ 06 — What this proves

Three principles.
2002 → 2026.

What a single filing-cabinet letter still teaches us — and the three principles AlwaysOutreach runs on, every day, in 2026.

i. The right people

Decision-makers do reply.

Chief executives of 170,000-person companies read their post. They reply when the message respects their time and names something specific. Not often. But reliably.

ii. The whole game

Research is the leverage.

The two bespoke paragraphs are the entire reason the rest gets read. Without them you are spam; with them you are a peer who has done the homework.

iii. The system

A good letter is a template.

The body — the pitch, the proof, the ask — is written once. It is reusable forever, with new names and a fresh top. That is the leverage AlwaysOutreach gives you.
